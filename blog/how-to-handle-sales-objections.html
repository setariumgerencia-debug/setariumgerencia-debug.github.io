How to Handle Sales Objections: 7 Common Ones and What to Say

Updated May 2026 · 7 min read

An objection isn't a rejection — it's a request for more information. The customers who object are often the ones most seriously considering buying. The goal is never to "win" the argument; it's to understand the real concern and address it honestly. Here are the seven you'll hear most, and how to respond.

1. "It's too expensive"

Acknowledge, then reframe to value: "Totally fair to weigh the cost. Most people make it back within [timeframe] because [outcome]. Want me to walk you through how that works for you?" Don't rush to discount — that signals the price was never real.

2. "I need to think about it"

Usually a hidden concern. Surface it gently: "Of course — what's the main thing you're weighing up? Happy to help you think it through." Often the real objection comes out here, and now you can address it.

3. "I need to ask my partner/boss"

Help them sell it internally: "Makes sense. What questions do you think they'll have? I can give you everything you need to make the case." You're arming your advocate, not pushing.

4. "Now isn't a good time"

Find out if it's timing or interest: "No problem — is it the timing specifically, or something about the offer? If it's timing, when would be better and I'll follow up then." This keeps the door open without pressure.

5. "I'm already using something else"

Don't bash the competitor. Ask: "Good to hear you've got a system. What's the one thing you wish it did better?" Their answer tells you exactly where you fit — or whether to walk away gracefully.

6. "I don't think it'll work for me"

Address the doubt with proof, not promises: "I get the hesitation. Here's how someone in a similar spot used it and what happened." A concrete example beats reassurance every time.

7. Silence (the unspoken objection)

When someone goes quiet, don't vanish. A light follow-up wins back more deals than any clever line: "Hey, still thinking it over? Happy to answer anything that's on your mind." Polite persistence is the most underrated sales skill.

Handle objections by listening first, acknowledging the concern, and responding honestly. You won't close everyone — and you shouldn't — but you'll close far more than someone who treats every objection as a fight.
