Excel vs Google Sheets for Small Business Accounting

Updated May 2026 · 6 min read

If you're tracking your business finances in a spreadsheet, the first question is which one. Excel and Google Sheets both do the job, but they shine in different situations. Here's an honest comparison so you can pick without second-guessing.

Cost

Google Sheets is free with any Google account. Excel comes with a Microsoft 365 subscription or a one-time desktop license. For a solo business or anyone watching costs, Sheets wins on price alone — and it's powerful enough for nearly all small-business accounting.

Collaboration

This is Google Sheets' biggest strength. Multiple people can edit the same file live, from any device, with no "final_v3_REAL.xlsx" email chaos. If you work with a partner, assistant, or accountant, real-time sharing is a genuine time-saver. Excel has co-authoring too, but it's smoother in Sheets.

Power and large data

Excel pulls ahead with very large datasets, advanced analysis (pivot tables, Power Query), and offline reliability. If you're crunching tens of thousands of rows or building complex models, Excel handles it better. For everyday income, expense, and invoice tracking, the difference rarely matters.

Offline use

Excel works offline by default. Google Sheets can work offline too, but it needs setup and is best with a connection. If you frequently work without internet, that's a point for Excel.

The honest verdict

For most small businesses: start with Google Sheets. It's free, collaborative, and more than capable. Reach for Excel if you need heavy data analysis or always work offline.
